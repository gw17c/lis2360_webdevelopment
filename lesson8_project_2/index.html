What is it? Define IoT Movement.

The internet of things is a new model of communication that conceptualizes a future where everyday objects will be assembled with digital communication transceivers, microcontrollers, and suitable protocol stacks. These devices will allow these objects to communicate with each other and the consumer. This movement will evolve into an essential component of the Internet (Zanella, Bui, Castellani, Vangelista, & Zorzi, 2014). In other words, this advanced technology allows appliances, vehicles and other objects to send and receive data through the internet using sensors.

The future of IoT is growing. Imagine the possibilities.

An IoT device could be turning on a light switch from your smartphone or even a car driving itself. You can have your refrigerator detect the expired foods and place an order to the grocery store on its own. The internet of things are these objects that used to depend on humans to operate can now operate on their own by connecting to the internet. A report analyzed that 8.4 billion IoT devices worldwide were used in 2017 and it is predicted to go up in 2020 to 20.4 billion. The movement has predicted to one day make almost everything connect to the internet.

Beneficial or Detrimental to Society? Hmm.

The IoT has many benefits for you, your home, an enterprise, utilities and mobiles. These benefits include smarter analytics, enhanced security, increased productivity, smart inventory, safer travel and real time demand visibility. IoT devices can provide benefits in the areas of transportation, agriculture, healthcare, entertainment, fitness and more. On the other hand, despite all the advancements and benefits to our society, it may also be very detrimental to society. It poses the question of whether security and privacy issues outweigh the benefits. These systems and devices that will be used within our homes and businesses collect a lot of data about you. An IoT device can potentially have information stored about a person's location, health, finances, shopping behavior and inventory (Singh & Singh, 2015).
